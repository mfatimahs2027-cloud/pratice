Reimagining Harlem Plaza

A redesign proposal focused on improving community experience, thermal comfort, movement, safety, and public activation.

Explore Project

Research & Context

Harlem Plaza, also known as Montefiore Square, is an important public space in Hamilton Heights that serves residents, subway commuters, students, small businesses, and visitors. This proposal studies how the plaza currently functions and identifies what still needs improvement after its 2022 renovation.

Site Analysis

Harlem Plaza (Montefiore Square) is a triangular public space in Hamilton Heights, West Harlem, bounded by Broadway, W 138th Street, and Hamilton Place. The 137th Street–City College 1-train stop is located at the southern edge of the plaza, while City College of New York sits one block to the northeast.

The site received a major $18.4 million renovation in 2022 that expanded its footprint and introduced 36 new trees. Even with these improvements, several issues remain unresolved: the open paved areas still lack overhead shade, seating options are limited, and the plaza does not provide enough reasons for people to stay and gather.

Approximately 40,000 residents live within a 10-minute walk of the plaza, making it one of the most accessible neighborhood public spaces in upper Manhattan.

Key Findings

The site analysis revealed three major problems. First, the lack of shade makes the plaza uncomfortable during summer afternoons, and the newly planted trees will take years to provide meaningful canopy coverage. Second, the fixed granite seat walls offer only one type of seating experience and do not support flexibility, comfort, or social interaction.

Third, the plaza has almost no programming infrastructure. There is no designated event area, no built-in support for temporary markets, and no strong features that encourage people to remain in the space. As a result, the plaza functions more as a pass-through zone than as a destination for community use.

Users & Problems

The redesign responds to the needs of several user groups who rely on the plaza in different ways. Each group experiences the site differently, but all are affected by the plaza’s current limitations in comfort, safety, and activation.

Primary Users

Local Residents

Hamilton Heights includes strong Dominican-American, West African, and African-American communities. Long-term residents need a plaza that feels welcoming, culturally grounded, and usable for daily life. Senior residents especially need shaded areas, ADA-friendly seating, and smooth pathways.

Commuters

The nearby 137th Street subway station creates heavy daily pedestrian traffic. Commuters mainly use the plaza as a circulation space, so they need clear paths, easy visibility, and safer crossings. Even short visits would improve with better shade and wayfinding.

Small Business Owners

Businesses along Broadway would benefit from a plaza that encourages people to linger rather than simply pass through. A more active plaza could support local vendors, increase foot traffic, and strengthen the relationship between the public space and the commercial edge.

Visitors

Visitors, including first-time neighborhood users and students from nearby City College, need a plaza that clearly communicates its local identity. The design should help them understand that this is a specific Harlem community space rather than a generic urban plaza.

Core Problems

Lack of Safe Pedestrian Movement

The triangular geometry of the plaza creates multiple crossing points where pedestrians and turning vehicles come into conflict. During rush periods, this becomes especially difficult for subway riders and students moving quickly through the area.

Insufficient Seating and Comfort Zones

The current granite seat walls are durable, but they provide only one fixed seating option. Users cannot move chairs into the shade, face each other socially, or choose where to sit based on comfort and activity.

Limited Visibility and Lighting

During the evening, the plaza depends mostly on standard street lighting. This leaves interior seating and gathering areas in shadow, reducing the sense of safety and making the plaza less usable after dark.

Underutilized Public Space

Although the renovation improved the physical layout, the plaza still lacks features that encourage staying. Without flexible seating, shade, and programming, most users spend only a short amount of time there before moving on.

Final Redesign Proposal

The final proposal combines the strongest ideas from the earlier explorations into one realistic redesign strategy for Harlem Plaza. The goal is not to completely rebuild the plaza, but to strengthen the 2022 renovation with additions that improve comfort, flexibility, and community use.

Design Strategy

The proposal introduces three major interventions. First, tensile shade sails are installed over the most active seating and circulation zones to provide immediate relief from summer heat while the young trees continue to grow. Second, moveable café-style seating and small tables are added to the central paved area so users can choose how they want to occupy the space.

Third, the redesign creates a flexible cultural activation zone for temporary markets, neighborhood events, student gatherings, and public programming. Together, these improvements transform the plaza from a space people move through into a place where people can pause, gather, and participate.
